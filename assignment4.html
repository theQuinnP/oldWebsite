Challenge 5: Accessibility Concerns

HTML webpages are massively based on navigation and understanding by the visuals provided, so for someone who is visually impaired, using these pages can be extremely difficult and counter intuitive. Web forms, for example, can be difficult to navigate due to the various input types. I used four different types, which may be confusing or misleading to someone who can’t see what the layout looks like. Keeping all my inputs as one type, such as checkboxes or text boxes could help to minimize this confusion. Additionally, some input types, such as the color picker, can’t be fully translated from visuals to words, making it nearly impossible for someone using a screen reader to answer. It is also very easy for those of us with good eyesight to overlook visual clues that may not translate to someone who has difficulties viewing a screen, such as bold/italic text to denote which areas of the form may be important or required, resulting in issues when they try to submit the form. This could be easily solved by making the instructions more clear, instead of relying on visual cues to get information across.
